R2 Benchmark AI Summary — c6in.16xlarge
========================================
Instance type : c6in.16xlarge
Data source   : results/capacity_check_r2_20260207_225453.parquet
Run wall-clock: 2026-02-07 22:39 – 22:54 (0.25 hours, ~15 minutes)
Total records : 77,902 logical requests (100% success rate, HTTP 200)
Total data    : 7,607.62 GB transferred
Avg throughput: 73.52 Gbps overall
Avg latency   : 4,158.7 ms  |  P50 3,723 ms  |  P95 8,490 ms  |  P99 10,607 ms
Concurrency   : 192 (warmup/ramp_1) → 384 (ramp_2) → 576 (ramp_3) → 768 (ramp_4)
Retries       : not recorded (no error_retry_report.txt; error_analysis.png shows HTTP 200 only)

CONTEXT: This is a ramp-only experiment — NO steady_state phase. Four ramp phases step through
concurrency 192 → 384 → 576 → 768. The c6in.16xlarge has a 50 Gbps network card but this
experiment achieves up to ~78 Gbps (likely through enhanced networking / ENA Express allowing
burst above nominal NIC limit). A notable throughput peak occurs at concurrency 384, with slight
decline at 576 and further at 768. No throttling cliff is observed.

=== CROSS-PLOT FINDINGS & KEY OBSERVATIONS ===

1. THROUGHPUT PEAK AT CONCURRENCY 384: The highest throughput (76.1 Gbps from throughput table,
   ~78.4 Gbps per phase stats) occurs at concurrency 384. Both lower (192: ~65 Gbps) and
   higher (576: ~74 Gbps, 768: ~75 Gbps) concurrencies deliver less. This defines a clear
   optimal concurrency for this instance type.

2. TWO-TIER THROUGHPUT STRUCTURE: The jump from 192→384 connections produces a large throughput
   gain (+~13 Gbps, ~20%). The 384→576→768 range shows only marginal further change (within
   ~4 Gbps). This suggests 384 is the minimum concurrency to saturate R2's available bandwidth
   for this instance, and adding more connections beyond that offers no further gain.

3. MONOTONIC LATENCY INCREASE: Mean latency at each step — 192: 1,841 ms → 384: 3,076 ms →
   576: 4,948 ms → 768: 6,991 ms — roughly doubles from the lowest to highest concurrency.
   This is strictly monotonic, unlike the throughput which peaks and then slowly declines.

4. HIGH ABSOLUTE THROUGHPUT: ~73.5 Gbps average and peak of ~78–80 Gbps exceeds the nominal
   50 Gbps NIC rating of the c6in.16xlarge. This is possible via ENA Express or temporary
   burst above the sustained limit. The c6in.16xlarge delivers the highest peak throughput of
   any instance tested in this series.

5. EFFICIENCY PENALTY: Per-connection efficiency collapses from 0.339 Gbps/connection at 192
   to 0.094 Gbps/connection at 768 — a ~72% reduction. At 768 connections, each connection
   contributes less than 0.1 Gbps despite total throughput being ~72 Gbps.

6. RTT ANOMALY AT HIGH CONCURRENCY: The throughput stats table shows RTT jumping from
   133 ms (ramp_1, 192 connections) to 647 ms (ramp_2, 384 connections) to 2,340 ms (ramp_3,
   576 connections) to 4,374 ms (ramp_4, 768 connections). This dramatic RTT increase at high
   concurrency suggests significant queueing or R2-side processing delays beyond simple transfer
   time, and is a key differentiator from the lower-concurrency results.

7. PERFECT RELIABILITY: 100% success rate across 77,902 requests and all four concurrency
   levels. No errors despite pushing to 768 concurrent connections at ~75 Gbps.

8. END-OF-RUN DRAIN ARTEFACT: Both timeline plots show a sharp throughput collapse to ~24 Gbps
   in the final 30-second window of ramp_4. This is consistent with run termination draining
   the in-flight queue, not a performance event.
