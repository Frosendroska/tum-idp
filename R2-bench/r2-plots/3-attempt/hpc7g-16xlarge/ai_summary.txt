=============================================================================
KEY FINDINGS AND INTERPRETATION
=============================================================================

1. INVERTED-U PEAKING AT 384 — WITH CAVEAT:
   Measured throughput: 192→41.1, 384→46.1 (peak), 576→37.3 Gbps.
   However, the 192 point is substantially deflated by the two severe throughput dips
   within ramp_1. Peak-period throughput at 192 concurrency is ~47–48 Gbps, nearly
   equal to ramp_2's 48.00 Gbps. The true optimal concurrency window is likely 192–384;
   the apparent inverted-U peak at 384 is partly an artifact of ramp_1 instability.

2. SEVERE RAMP_1 THROUGHPUT DIPS:
   Two distinct throughput collapses occurred during ramp_1 (192 concurrency), each
   dropping from ~48 Gbps to ~10 Gbps and lasting ~20–30 seconds, totaling ~90–120
   seconds of severely depressed throughput within the 207.7-second phase. Corresponding
   latency plumes (25,000–45,000 ms) are visible in the scatter/rolling-average plots.
   Cause is unclear — possibly momentary TCP/connection stalls, R2-side throttling, or
   a burst of slow-server responses clogging all 192 pipelines simultaneously.
   Ramp_2 and ramp_3 do not exhibit similar structured dips (they have noise but no
   step-change collapses), making this a ramp_1-specific anomaly.

3. RTT ESCALATION WITH CONCURRENCY:
   Phase RTT: 162.7 ms (warmup/192) → 138.8 ms (ramp_1/192) → 684.2 ms (ramp_2/384)
   → 2,961.1 ms (ramp_3/576).
   RTT nearly 5× between 384 and 576, and ~21× between 192 and 576. This indicates
   significant server-side queueing or network congestion at 576 connections.
   Contrasts with c6in-32xlarge runs 2 and 3 which showed flat RTT even at 2,304
   connections. Suggests the hpc7g/hpc7g instance's network stack or R2 endpoint
   begins to saturate around 384–576 connections.

4. MONOTONIC LATENCY INCREASE:
   Mean latency nearly triples from 192 (2,504 ms) to 576 (7,229 ms), scaling
   approximately linearly with concurrency. This combined with the RTT escalation
   indicates the system is not absorbing additional connections at 576; performance
   degrades significantly past 384.

5. LARGE OBJECT SIZE (~97.6 MB):
   Similar to c6in-32xlarge run 1 (~97.7 MB) and r8gd-4xlarge (~97.6 MB objects).
   Larger objects mean longer per-request TCP transfer times, which amplifies the
   impact of latency outliers and pipeline stalls.

6. EFFICIENCY PROFILE:
   0.214 Gbps/conn at 192, dropping to 0.065 at 576. A ~3.3× efficiency loss over
   a 3× concurrency increase. This is steeper than typical (ideal would be flat or
   gentle decline), confirming that connections beyond 192 are increasingly wasteful.

7. NAMING DISCREPANCY:
   Folder: hpc7g-16xlarge. Parquet source: hpc7g-capacity_check_r2_20260208_000827.
   hpc7g.16xlarge and hpc7g.16xlarge are different AWS instance types (hpc7g is
   Graviton-based ARM; hpc7g is AMD-based). The actual instance used should be
   verified against AWS billing/CloudWatch records.

8. 100% SUCCESS RATE, NO RETRIES:
   Despite throughput instability in ramp_1, all 36,072 requests eventually completed
   successfully. The dips represent throughput degradation, not request failures.
